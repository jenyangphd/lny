Empire, Migration, and the Bakery Counter

Every item in this collection exists because something moved — people, seeds, techniques, or capital — across a border that someone else drew. Chili peppers crossed the Pacific via Portuguese trade ships and became gochujang. American wheat flooded postwar Japan and became shokupan. Persimmons left China for Japan, Korea, and eventually California, carried by immigrants whose labor made them grow. Fortune cookies traveled from Kyoto to San Francisco to every Chinese restaurant in America, changing identity at each stop.

The common thread is not simply "Asian food" but the specific forces that shape what people eat and what meaning they attach to it: colonial trade routes, wartime occupation, exclusion laws, forced migration, and the economic pressures that push people to adapt, improvise, and invent. Hong Kong's pineapple buns exist because British colonialism brought ovens, Portuguese Macau brought egg tarts, and Chinese-Mexican returnees brought conchas.

The modern Asian bakery is where all of these histories converge. What connects these foods is not a shared cuisine but a shared condition: they were all shaped by forces larger than any single culture, and they were all made meaningful by the people who received, adapted, and loved them. These foods are authentic to the histories that produced them, which are histories of encounter, displacement, and invention under constraint. The bakery counter is where empire's ingredients meet immigrant ingenuity, and the result is something that belongs fully to no single tradition, and entirely to the communities that sustain it.
