Persimmon Tartlets with Black Sesame Pâte Sablée

Two ancient ingredients, one very presumptuous French crust.

The persimmon has been cultivated in China for 2,000 years and was introduced to Japan in the 7th century and Korea by the 14th century. Across East Asia, the persimmon is a seasonal marker, a ritual object, an artistic motif, and a vehicle for linguistic wordplay.

The history of black sesame goes back even further—to at least 2000 BCE. Across East Asian cuisines, black sesame is simultaneously medicinal and indulgent.

Fun Fact

In China, the persimmon means “may things go your way.” In Korea, folktales say it wards off tigers. In Japan, it inspired one of the most well-known haikus ever written (by Masaoka Shiki).

Not-So-Fun Fact

Commodore Matthew Perry is often credited with bringing persimmons to the U.S. But it was actually Japanese immigrants who made the fruit commercially viable in California’s climate. This mirrors a broader pattern of colonial or military figures receiving credit for “discovery,” while the labor of immigrant communities that actually cultivated, adapted, and sustained these foods goes unrecognized.
