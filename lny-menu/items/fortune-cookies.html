Fortune Cookies

A persistent case study in how diasporic food identities form.

The fortune cookie traces back to tsujiura senbei, a Japanese cracker made with sesame and miso in the 1870s. Japanese immigrants brought the tradition to California in the 1880s, where bakeries adapted the recipe with vanilla and butter for American palates. When Japanese Americans were forcibly interned during WWII, their businesses were seized or shuttered, opening the door for Chinese-American producers to adopt the cookie, which became indelibly tied to Chinese restaurants.

Fun Fact

The world’s largest producer of fortune cookies is headquartered in Brooklyn! The company, Wonton Food, tried to sell fortune cookies to China in 1992, and they flopped.

Not-So-Fun Fact

The fortune cookie also functioned as a cultural bridge, as many Japanese immigrants opened Chinese restaurants because Americans at the time didn’t want to eat Japanese food, but embraced chop suey-era Chinese cuisine.
