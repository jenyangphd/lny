Cream Puffs with Hong Kong Milk Tea Crème Diplomat

France piped the shell. Britain steeped the tea. Hong Kong made it lunch.

Choux pastry is one of the foundational doughs of French pâtisserie (alongside pâte brisée, pâte feuilletée, and pâte sablée). Choux pastry traveled to Japan in the 1800s via Yokohama’s foreign concession, and shu cream made its commercial debut in Tokyo’s Kanda district.

Hong Kong milk tea originated in the mid-20th century as a local working-class adaptation of British afternoon tea, using stronger tea blends and evaporated milk. HK milk tea is inseparable from cha chaan teng—a hybrid café that serves a distinctly Hong Kong fusion of Western and Chinese food. The tea is always served alongside this in-between cuisine.

Fun Fact

Hong Kong milk tea is also called “silk stocking” tea, named for the original brewing method: a woman tailored cotton interlock fabric used for suit jacket linings into a bag-shaped filter to produce a smooth brew (weird that people only remember her husband’s name..)

Not-So-Fun Fact

Britain’s insatiable demand for Chinese tea created a massive trade deficit, which Britain “solved” by flooding China with opium. The resulting Opium Wars led to the cession of Hong Kong in 1842. The milk tea in these cream puffs exists because of a colonial narcotics operation.
